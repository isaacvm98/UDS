Early Evidence on Generative AI Exposure and Labor Market Outcomes¶

Team Members: Aesha Gandhi, Pinaki Ghosh, Shambhavi Khanna, Isaac Vergara
Stakeholder: Director of Workforce Policy, State Department of Labor
Repository: GitHub Repository

Stakeholder Summary¶

This memo is written for a Director of Workforce Policy at a state Department of Labor. The intended stakeholder is assumed to be comfortable interpreting applied research and labor market indicators, but not interested in econometric detail. The main priority is actionable evidence that can inform workforce planning and early-career employment policy.

Executive Summary¶

This memo examines whether workers in industries with higher exposure to generative AI experienced different labor market outcomes after the arrival of GenAI tools, and whether those patterns were more pronounced for younger workers. The analysis uses CPS ASEC data from 2016-2025, links those records to an industry-level AI exposure measure (AIIE), and compares how employment outcomes changed after the GenAI period began.

The clearest pattern is a modest worsening in unemployment outcomes after 2023 for workers in higher-AI-exposure industries. That shift appears more pronounced for workers ages 25-34 than for older workers. By contrast, no comparable wage effect is visible in the data over this short post period.

These results should be interpreted with caution. The unemployment findings are more informative than the wage findings, but neither result should be treated as definitive proof of causality. Even so, the evidence points to a practical concern for this stakeholder: labor market conditions may be softening somewhat more for younger workers in AI-exposed sectors, which makes those workers an appropriate focus for monitoring and policy attention.

Decisions To Be Made¶

Based on these findings, three near-term decisions appear especially relevant:

  1. Whether to prioritize monitoring and support for younger workers in AI-exposed sectors, especially workers in the 25-34 range.
  2. Whether to expand labor-market monitoring so that hiring, job-finding, and layoffs can be separated more clearly in future work.
  3. Whether to treat wage changes as a secondary indicator and focus policy attention first on employment outcomes.

The Problem¶

Public debate about generative AI often revolves around questions of job displacement or wage depression by artificial intelligence. From the perspective of a labor market policymaker, however, a more pertinent question might be limited to whether workers in more exposed industries are experiencing worse labor market outcomes, especially among younger workers.

Importantly, the study analyzes labor force participation rates among workers aged 25-34, a population that represents recent entrants into the workforce, as well as older workers who have multiple years of work experience and may be encountering less hiring, layoffs, or even stagnating careers due to exposure to artificial intelligence. The issue, therefore, extends beyond simply entering the labor market; it concerns whether labor force participation rates are declining faster among early-career workers in industries affected by artificial intelligence than other, older workers. The Current Population Survey Annual Social and Economic Supplement does not have a separate variable measuring layoffs.

Analytical Approach¶

To study this question, CPS ASEC data from 2016-2025 were merged with an industry-level AI exposure measure, AIIE. The analysis then compares how unemployment and wage outcomes changed after the GenAI period began in industries with higher AI exposure versus industries with lower AI exposure.

The memo focuses on two outcomes:

  • Unemployment
  • Wage income

The analysis also looks separately at younger workers ages 25-34 to assess whether labor market changes appear more concentrated earlier in workers' careers.

Examples of relatively high-AI-exposure industries in this sample include legal services, accounting and payroll services, computer systems design, and management consulting. Lower-exposure examples include landscaping, services to buildings and dwellings, warehousing and storage, and truck transportation. These examples are drawn from industries near the top and bottom of the AI exposure distribution in the sample, and are intended to give a concrete sense of what higher- and lower-exposure industries look like in practice.

More emphasis is placed on the unemployment results because they are more consistent with the assumptions needed for this type of comparison. The wage results are still useful descriptively, but they are less persuasive as evidence of a distinct AI-related shift.

Main Findings¶

1. Higher AI exposure is associated with a relative increase in unemployment after 2023¶

The main unemployment comparison suggests that unemployment outcomes worsened slightly more after 2023 in higher-AI-exposure industries than in lower-exposure industries.

This is a modest effect, not a dramatic one. It also does not mean exposed industries became high-unemployment industries overall. In the raw data, those industries still tend to have lower unemployment than less-exposed industries. The key point is that their advantage narrowed in the post period.

2. The unemployment shift is larger for workers ages 25-34¶

The age comparison suggests that the post-period unemployment shift is larger for workers ages 25-34 than for workers ages 35-64.

That pattern matters because it suggests the labor market impact is not evenly spread across age groups. At the same time, the analysis does not identify the exact mechanism. The safest conclusion is that younger workers in higher-AI-exposure industries experienced relatively weaker unemployment outcomes after 2023.

Notably, the observed gap between the two cohorts is more than just an interesting trend seen in the data; it is sufficiently large to be assured that it is unlikely to occur by chance. The results show that the change in unemployment since 2023 is significantly greater for workers aged 25-34 compared to those aged 35-64, controlling for age and educational attainment by industry.

3. The youngest subgroup shows the largest point estimate, but the age split is not conclusive¶

When the younger group is split further, the estimated effect is larger for ages 25-29 than for ages 30-34. However, that difference is not statistically precise enough to support a strong claim that the youngest subgroup is distinctly more affected.

The age-split evidence is therefore best treated as suggestive. It points toward somewhat greater vulnerability among the youngest workers in the sample, but it does not establish a sharp divide within the 25-34 group.

In considering the division of the young generation into workers aged 25-29 and workers aged 30-34, the effect seems somewhat greater for the latter. Nevertheless, the discrepancy between the two groups is not large enough to suggest anything other than statistical fluctuations that may have occurred in the course of the experiment. It seems best to consider that the effect occurs somewhat more clearly in the case of workers aged 25-29, while no line should yet be drawn between them.

4. No meaningful wage effect is detected¶

The wage analysis does not show evidence that wages changed differently after 2024 in higher-AI-exposure industries.

This should not be read as proof that AI has no wage effects. The post period is still short, and wage adjustments may take longer to appear. The wage analysis is also the weaker comparison in this memo because its pre-period patterns are less stable.

Why the Unemployment Result Is More Informative Than the Wage Result¶

In order to be confident that the post-2023 divergence in unemployment was not merely a result of pre-existing trends, one needs to establish whether there were any signs of growing apart between higher- and lower-AI-exposed industries before the generative AI was popularized. Here, the logic is simple, if the two industries were already diverging before 2023, one cannot confidently claim that their separation is caused by AI. On the other hand, if the two industries were moving alike and only started diverging after 2023, such results are likely caused by AI.

This test proves rather positive for unemployment but not quite for wage. In most years before 2023, higher- and lower-AI-exposure industries behaved almost alike in terms of unemployment. As for wages, higher-AI-exposed industries had been earning more money long before the arrival of generative AI. Thus, in terms of wages, the current results are hardly convincing enough since any increase in wages is hardly attributed to AI but rather to something else. This explains the rationale behind paying more attention to unemployment data rather than wages in this memo.

Robustness¶

The younger-worker unemployment pattern remains visible after dropping 2020, the most unusual COVID year. That reduces concern that the main result is being driven entirely by pandemic volatility.

At the same time, the broader caution remains the same: these checks improve confidence that the pattern is not a one-year anomaly, but they do not turn the analysis into definitive causal proof.

Limitations¶

This analysis has a few important limitations.

First, the CPS is a repeated cross-section, not a panel, so the same worker cannot be followed over time. The results therefore describe changes in group-level outcomes rather than individual trajectories.

Second, AIIE measures potential AI exposure based on industry task content, not actual AI adoption by a specific employer.

Third, the data in this case do not differentiate between whether workers were laid off because of involuntary conditions or they chose to leave their employment for whatever reason. Therefore, the results of unemployment may be as a consequence of either hiring being lower or firing being higher, or both.

Fourth, the post period is still short, especially for wages.

Finally, the supporting diagnostic checks are more favorable for unemployment than for wages, but they are not strong enough to remove all concern about pre-existing differences across industries.

Conclusion¶

The main takeaway is that labor market conditions appear to have weakened somewhat more after 2023 for younger workers in higher-AI-exposure industries. The unemployment comparison points in that direction, and the age breakdown suggests the pattern is stronger for workers ages 25-34 than for older workers.

That said, the memo should not be read as definitive proof that generative AI caused these changes. The evidence is better viewed as an early warning signal. For this stakeholder, the most practical implication is that younger workers in AI-exposed sectors warrant closer monitoring, while wage effects are not yet clearly visible in the data.

Appendix B. Analysis Setup and Variable Construction¶

The next blocks reproduce the final causal analysis setup, including reproducibility settings, sample restrictions, variable definitions, and helper functions used throughout the analysis.

Analysis Frame and Key Variables¶

The final analysis uses CPS ASEC repeated cross-sections from 2016-2025 merged with industry-level AI exposure, AIIE. The analytical frame applies the following restrictions and variable definitions:

  • Working-age sample: ages 25-64
  • Exposure requirement: non-missing AIIE
  • Unemployment sample: individuals in the labor force
  • Wage sample: employed individuals with positive wage income
  • Main wage DiD sample: same as the wage sample, excluding 2023 as a transition year
  • Survey weights: ASECWT
  • Clustered standard errors: by IND_2022
  • post_unemployment = 1 for YEAR >= 2023
  • post_wage = 1 for YEAR >= 2024
  • early_career = 1 for ages 25-34
  • log_wage = log(INCWAGE) for positive wage observations

For plots only, AIIE is also split into low- and high-exposure groups using the 25th and 75th percentiles. The regressions use continuous AIIE, not the grouped version.

Appendix C. Core Estimation Code¶

This section reproduces the main estimation workflow from the causal analysis notebook. It starts with the model specifications, then shows the sample checks, descriptive plots, event-study diagnostics, main difference-in-differences regressions, and age-heterogeneity specifications.

Difference-in-Differences Specifications¶

The baseline unemployment difference-in-differences model is:

$$ \begin{aligned} unemployed_{it} &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 AIIE_j + \beta_2 post^{unemp}_t \\ &\quad + \beta_3 \left(AIIE_j \times post^{unemp}_t\right) + \beta_4 AGE_i \\ &\quad + \sum_e \delta_e 1\{EDUC_i = e\} + \varepsilon_{it} \end{aligned} $$

The unemployment heterogeneity model adds a triple interaction:

$$ \begin{aligned} unemployed_{it} &= \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 AIIE_j + \alpha_2 post^{unemp}_t + \alpha_3 early\_career_i \\ &\quad + \alpha_4 \left(AIIE_j \times post^{unemp}_t\right) \\ &\quad + \alpha_5 \left(AIIE_j \times early\_career_i\right) \\ &\quad + \alpha_6 \left(post^{unemp}_t \times early\_career_i\right) \\ &\quad + \alpha_7 \left(AIIE_j \times post^{unemp}_t \times early\_career_i\right) \\ &\quad + \alpha_8 AGE_i + \sum_e \rho_e 1\{EDUC_i = e\} + u_{it} \end{aligned} $$

The baseline wage difference-in-differences model is:

$$ \begin{aligned} \log(wage_{it}) &= \gamma_0 + \gamma_1 AIIE_j + \gamma_2 post^{wage}_t \\ &\quad + \gamma_3 \left(AIIE_j \times post^{wage}_t\right) + \gamma_4 AGE_i \\ &\quad + \sum_e \kappa_e 1\{EDUC_i = e\} + \nu_{it} \end{aligned} $$

The wage heterogeneity model parallels the unemployment triple-interaction setup:

$$ \begin{aligned} \log(wage_{it}) &= \eta_0 + \eta_1 AIIE_j + \eta_2 post^{wage}_t + \eta_3 early\_career_i \\ &\quad + \eta_4 \left(AIIE_j \times post^{wage}_t\right) \\ &\quad + \eta_5 \left(AIIE_j \times early\_career_i\right) \\ &\quad + \eta_6 \left(post^{wage}_t \times early\_career_i\right) \\ &\quad + \eta_7 \left(AIIE_j \times post^{wage}_t \times early\_career_i\right) \\ &\quad + \eta_8 AGE_i + \sum_e \phi_e 1\{EDUC_i = e\} + \omega_{it} \end{aligned} $$

Variable definitions:

  • $unemployed_{it}$: indicator equal to 1 if individual $i$ is unemployed in year $t$
  • $\log(wage_{it})$: log wage income for individual $i$ in year $t$
  • $AIIE_j$: AI Industry Exposure for industry $j$
  • $post^{unemp}_t$: indicator equal to 1 in 2023-2025
  • $post^{wage}_t$: indicator equal to 1 in 2024-2025
  • $early\_career_i$: indicator equal to 1 for ages 25-34
  • $AGE_i$: age in years
  • $1\{EDUC_i = e\}$: education-category controls

All models are estimated using CPS ASEC weights and cluster-robust standard errors at the 2022 Census industry level.

Why Check Parallel Trends?¶

The descriptive figures are useful for showing the broad pattern in the data, but they are not enough on their own to justify a difference-in-differences interpretation. The key identifying assumption behind the DiD design is that, absent the post-2022 expansion of generative AI, outcomes in higher- and lower-exposure industries would have continued to move along similar underlying paths.

This is why we now dive into an event-study version of the model. The event-study check asks whether the relationship between AIIE and the outcome was already shifting before the post period began. If those pre-period differences are small, that makes the DiD setup more credible. If they are large or systematic, then the post-period DiD estimates are harder to interpret causally because some of the gap may reflect pre-existing divergence rather than a post-2022 change.

Parallel Trends and Event-Study Diagnostic¶

Parallel trends are evaluated with an event-study specification that replaces the single post-period interaction with year-specific interactions between AIIE and survey year:

$$ \begin{aligned} Y_{it} &= \theta_0 + \sum_{\tau \neq 2022} \theta_{\tau} \left(AIIE_j \times 1\{YEAR_t = \tau\}\right) \\ &\quad + \gamma_j + \sum_{\tau \neq 2022} \lambda_{\tau} 1\{YEAR_t = \tau\} \\ &\quad + \sum_k \psi_k X_{kit} + \varepsilon_{it} \end{aligned} $$

where $Y_{it}$ is either unemployment or log wage income, and $X_{kit}$ includes age and education controls. This is not a staggered-adoption design. The omitted year is 2022, so each coefficient shows how the relationship between AIIE and the outcome differs relative to 2022.

Interpretation of the diagnostic:

  • If pre-period coefficients are close to zero, that supports the parallel-trends assumption.
  • If pre-period coefficients are systematically different from zero, that suggests pre-existing differential trends.
  • The joint pre-trend test evaluates whether all pre-period year × AIIE terms are zero together.

Neither outcome fully passes this diagnostic. The unemployment specification still looks more informative than the wage specification because its pre-period deviations are smaller and less systematic, but it should still be treated as suggestive rather than definitive.

Interpreting the Age-Heterogeneity Test¶

This specification separates the younger portion of the sample into workers ages 25-29 and 30-34, using workers ages 35-64 as the reference group. The interaction coefficients in age_split_table show whether the post-2022 unemployment relationship with AIIE is larger for each younger subgroup than for the reference group.

The age_gap_test output is the key comparison between the two younger subgroups. A positive difference means the 25-29 estimate is larger than the 30-34 estimate. In these results, the point estimate for ages 25-29 is larger, but the formal Wald test indicates that the two subgroup effects are not statistically different from one another. The most careful interpretation is that the unemployment effect appears somewhat stronger for the youngest workers, but the analysis does not provide strong enough evidence to claim that the 25-29 group is affected differently from the 30-34 group in a statistically distinct way.
